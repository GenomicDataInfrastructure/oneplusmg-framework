# Preconception carrier screening  
*(Reproductive carrier screening)*

## On this page
- [Introduction](#introduction)
- [Key questions and considerations](#key-questions-and-considerations)

## Introduction

When couples or individuals are planning to have children, they may choose to learn more about their genetic makeup before conception. Through preconception genetic carrier screening (PGCS), it is possible to test whether one or both partners carry genetic variants that, on their own, do not cause health problems, but that — when combined — could increase the risk of certain inherited conditions in their children.

The aim of PGCS is to provide prospective parents with information that may help them better understand potential genetic risks and consider their reproductive options.

At present, PGCS is not yet standard practice in most European countries, although this may change as technologies develop and awareness increases.

## Key questions and considerations

As PGCS becomes more widely available, a number of important ethical and societal questions arise.

Should PGCS be offered to everyone as part of public healthcare, or only to specific groups, such as people undergoing fertility treatment or those with known genetic risks? Which conditions should be included in screening panels, and should these differ between countries or communities where certain inherited diseases are more common?

There are also important questions about access and fairness. How can societies ensure that screening is available to all, regardless of financial means, education or cultural background? And how can people be supported to make informed and voluntary decisions, without feeling social or moral pressure to participate?

Addressing these questions will be essential to ensure that the use of preconception carrier screening supports personal autonomy, equity and responsible decision-making.

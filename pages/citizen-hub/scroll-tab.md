    <section id="genomics-and-vulnerability">
      <h2>Genomics and vulnerability</h2>
      <p>Genomic data reveals inherent biological vulnerabilities that must be carefully managed. Understanding these risks is essential for developing equitable healthcare policies and protecting individuals from potential misuse of their genetic information.</p>
      <p>The intersection of genomics and personal vulnerability raises profound questions about how societies manage sensitive biological data in an era of rapidly advancing sequencing technologies.</p>
    </section>

    <section id="inherent-vulnerabilities">
      <h2>Inherent vulnerabilities and susceptibility</h2>
      <p>Certain populations carry heightened susceptibility to diseases due to genetic factors. These vulnerabilities require targeted support mechanisms and awareness to ensure no group is left without appropriate care or protection.</p>
      <p>Understanding inherited susceptibility allows healthcare providers to develop personalised approaches, but also creates risks if such information is accessed without proper consent frameworks in place.</p>
    </section>

    <section id="rare-diseases">
      <h2>Rare diseases and specific challenges</h2>
      <p>Rare disease patients face a unique set of challenges including limited treatment availability, high therapy costs, and reduced research investment. Addressing these gaps requires coordinated international efforts and policy intervention.</p>
      <p>The genomic basis of many rare diseases means that advances in sequencing and data sharing could dramatically improve diagnosis timelines and open pathways to novel therapeutic approaches.</p>
    </section>

    <section id="privacy-risks">
      <h2>Privacy risks</h2>
      <p>The collection and storage of genomic data introduces serious privacy concerns. Without robust safeguards, individuals risk exposure to discrimination by insurers, employers, or other parties who may misuse sensitive biological information.</p>
      <p>Data minimisation principles, strong encryption, and clear consent frameworks are among the essential tools for managing genomic privacy at scale.</p>
    </section>

    <section id="discrimination">
      <h2>Discrimination and stigmatisation</h2>
      <p>Genetic information can become a basis for discrimination and social stigma. Legal frameworks and public education are critical to preventing unfair treatment of individuals based on their genomic profile.</p>
      <p>Several jurisdictions have introduced genetic non-discrimination legislation, though gaps remain in coverage and enforcement across different sectors and countries.</p>
    </section>

    <section id="protection">
      <h2>Protection and empowerment</h2>
      <p>Empowering individuals with knowledge about their genomic data — alongside strong legal protections — ensures people can make informed decisions while being shielded from exploitation or harm.</p>
      <p>Patient advocacy groups, data trusts, and participatory governance models are emerging as important mechanisms for giving individuals meaningful control over their genetic information.</p>
    </section>

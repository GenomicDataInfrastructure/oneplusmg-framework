## Introduction

Genomics can provide valuable information about people’s health, their relatives and even future children. This knowledge can help guide medical decisions, improve treatments and support prevention. However, the results of genomic tests are not always straightforward or easy to interpret, and they may not always match people’s expectations.

Uncertainty is therefore an inherent part of genomics and needs to be understood and managed carefully.

## Uncertain results

When a genetic variant is identified, it may indicate an increased risk of developing a certain condition. However, for many variants, the impact on health is **not yet known**. In these cases, clinicians cannot determine whether a variant is harmless or whether it is associated with a real health risk.

These findings are often referred to as **variants of unknown significance (VUS)**. Scientific research may provide more clarity over time, but this requires extensive data and long-term study. In the meantime, uncertain results raise important questions, such as:

- should these findings be shared with patients?
- could they cause unnecessary worry or distress?
- do healthcare professionals have a responsibility to recheck these variants as new knowledge emerges and re-contact patients if interpretations change?

There is no simple or universal answer to these questions, and approaches vary across healthcare systems.

## Unexpected findings

Genomic testing can also produce **unexpected results**. A person may undergo testing for a specific reason, such as investigating a particular illness or through family (cascade) screening, but the test may reveal an increased risk for an unrelated condition.

While this information can enable prevention or early detection, it can also be distressing or overwhelming. As a result, there has been significant debate about:

- whether people should be able to choose whether they receive such findings
- how informed consent should be handled
- whether clinicians have a duty to disclose information that falls outside the original reason for testing

Different countries and healthcare systems take different approaches to managing these situations.

## The role of genetic counselling

Uncertain and unexpected results highlight the importance of **genetic counselling**. Counselling can help people understand their results, put risks into context and consider possible next steps.

However, counselling is delivered in many different ways across countries, and in some cases there is limited regulation or formal guidance. This can lead to variation in quality, access and consistency of support, even though effective counselling is essential for helping people make informed decisions.

## Avoiding genetic determinism

Correct interpretation of genomic information is also important to avoid **genetic determinism** — the idea that genes alone determine health or future outcomes.

While genomics offers significant medical and societal benefits, over-promising or placing too much emphasis on genetic factors can be misleading. Health is shaped by many factors, including lifestyle, environment and social conditions. Presenting genomics as one part of this broader picture helps ensure that citizens have a realistic and trustworthy understanding of what genomics can and cannot offer.
